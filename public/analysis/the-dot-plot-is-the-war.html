FOMC Oil Iran War Part 3 March 18, 2026 · 8 min read

The Dot Plot Is the War

What tomorrow's FOMC tells us about the next decade

Tuesday's numbers told the story the market doesn't want to hear. S&P up 0.25%. Not a crash, not a rally. Just... nothing. The relief rally from Monday — S&P up 1.2%, remember — burned off in a single session. Oil clawed back 3%, with Brent settling at $103.42. Iran attacked UAE's Fujairah port for the third time in four days, igniting a fire at the export terminal that handles roughly 1% of global demand.

And France formally said it would "never take part in operations to unblock the Strait of Hormuz."

Never. Not "we're considering it." Not "we need to consult allies." Never.

The escort coalition that Monday's rally was priced on? It doesn't just not exist — it's being actively rejected by every country asked to join.

Why Tomorrow's Dot Plot Is the Most Important in a Decade

The Fed decision itself is boring. Hold at 3.50-3.75%. CME FedWatch has this at 92%+. Nobody cares.

What everyone should care about — and what most retail investors will ignore — is the Summary of Economic Projections. The dot plot. The quarterly ritual where each FOMC member anonymously marks where they think rates will be at the end of 2026 and 2027.

Here's why this one is different from every dot plot since 2019:

The Core Tension

The Fed is staring at a genuine stagflationary impulse for the first time in the post-COVID era. February CPI came in at 2.4% — manageable. But oil above $100 feeds through to headline inflation with a 2-3 month lag. The war is entering its third week with Hormuz still effectively closed, UAE output halved, and Middle East crude benchmarks at all-time highs.

The December dot plot showed a median expectation of one 25bp cut in 2026. One cut. That was the market's baseline coming into the year. Now the question is whether that single dot moves up — signaling zero cuts — or stays put.

Three members shifting their dots up eliminates the cut entirely. Three members. Out of nineteen.

The Stagflation Trap

The standard macro playbook says: geopolitical shock → flight to safety → rate cuts to cushion growth. That's what the market wants. That's what the relief rally was pricing in — a Fed that sees the war as a growth headwind and responds with easier money.

But the Fed doesn't have that option when oil is at $103.

Cut rates with oil-driven inflation feeding through to CPI, and you lose the 2% target you spent three years clawing back to. You also tell every energy trader on Earth that the Fed will accommodate supply-shock inflation — which means oil goes higher, not lower.

Hold rates with a weakening economy, and you accept slower growth. Corporate earnings take a hit. The AI CapEx boom, which requires cheap capital and cheap energy, starts looking fragile.

There is no clean answer. That's what makes it a trap.

The last time the Fed faced a genuine oil-driven stagflationary shock was the 1970s. Volcker's response — crushing rates to 20% — worked, but it caused two recessions and unemployment above 10%. Nobody thinks we're headed there. But the logic is the same: you cannot cut rates into a supply shock without making the supply shock worse.

The Coalition That France Killed

Updated escort coalition scorecard:

  • Japan: PM Takaichi confirmed no formal request received. Not deploying.
  • Germany: Defense minister said "this is not our war, we have not started it."
  • Australia: Ruled out naval deployment.
  • France: President Macron said France would "never take part" during hostilities.
  • South Korea: "Carefully considering." Diplomatic for no.
  • China: No comment. They're buying discounted Iranian crude.
  • UK: Might send drone mine hunters. Might.

This isn't a coalition forming slowly. This is a coalition that has been publicly and definitively rejected by every major ally. France didn't leave the door open. They welded it shut.

Kevin Hassett told CNBC that tankers are "starting to dribble through." Dribble. That's the White House's best spin — a trickle of oil through a chokepoint that normally handles 20% of global supply.

The Bits-to-Atoms Rotation Is Accelerating

Part 2 flagged the structural rotation from digital to physical. Tuesday's data reinforced it:

  • ExxonMobil: Up 25%+ since January. The war is a revenue event for integrated oil majors.
  • Caterpillar and industrials: Surging on infrastructure demand.
  • Salesforce: Down 26% YTD. Enterprise SaaS most exposed to AI cannibalization and rising energy costs.
  • Microsoft: Under pressure despite strong earnings. Investors questioning $650B AI CapEx when energy costs are climbing and your data centers might be military targets.

The market is doing what markets do: repricing from narrative to reality. For three years, the narrative was "AI will eat everything, buy anything that touches software, ignore the physical world." That thesis assumed two things: cheap energy and geopolitical stability. Both are gone.

This isn't a sector rotation. It's a regime change. The last time energy and industrials outperformed tech for a sustained period was 2000-2008. That cycle lasted eight years.

What to Watch Tomorrow at 2 PM

Forget the rate decision. Here's what actually matters:

Four signals from the FOMC

1
The median 2026 dot. Currently shows one 25bp cut. If it moves to zero cuts, the market reprices everything — mortgage rates, corporate borrowing, AI CapEx plans, housing.
2
The inflation projection. December SEP showed 2026 PCE at 2.5%. If this moves above 2.7%, the Fed is telling you the oil shock feeds through structurally.
3
Powell's language on oil. Key word: "transitory." If he uses it, the market rallies and he's wrong. If he says "monitoring supply-side dynamics" — Fedspeak for "we can't fix this" — the market sells.
4
Dissents. A hawkish dissent — someone wanting to hike — would be the loudest signal possible. Unlikely but not impossible given $103 Brent.

The Decade-Long View

Here's the uncomfortable synthesis of everything in Parts 1, 2, and 3:

We are watching a real-time repricing of the assumption that the physical world doesn't matter. For twenty years, the dominant investment thesis has been: atoms are boring, bits are interesting. Every smart person went into software. Every smart dollar went into AI. Physical infrastructure — energy, shipping, industrial capacity, military logistics — was background noise.

The Iran war didn't create a new world. It revealed the one we were already living in. A world where 20% of global oil transits a strait 3 miles from a hostile coastline. Where data centers are military targets. Where no allied nation will risk its navy to keep the lights on. Where the Fed can't cut rates because a war 6,000 miles away is feeding through to CPI.

The dot plot tomorrow isn't just a rate signal. It's a thermometer for how quickly the world's most powerful economic institution is updating its model of reality.

Watch the dots. They'll tell you whether the Fed still believes this is a blip — or whether they've started pricing in the decade ahead.
